# Texas Land & Boundary Law — Compliance Notes for a 7-Module Boundary Course

**Purpose.** Reference notes used to make a 7-module boundary-law course **Texas-specific and accurate as of 2026**. Each topic gives (a) the rule, (b) key statutes/cases, and (c) a **"Texas vs. common law"** callout — the most valuable part for adapting general boundary-law material to Texas.

**How to use.** Organized by course module. Inline citations use the form *Case Name*, reporter cite (court year) and statute cites by code section. Citation-confidence flags (⚠️) mark items to confirm verbatim against the official source (`statutes.capitol.texas.gov`, `pels.texas.gov`, GLO, or the reporter) before quoting word-for-word in published materials. Nothing here is speculation; uncertainty is flagged explicitly.

**Verification basis.** Statute section numbers and operative content were cross-checked across the Texas codification and authoritative mirrors (texas.public.law, Justia, FindLaw, Cornell LII, tx.elaws.us). Case holdings were verified against Justia / CourtListener / FindLaw / vLex. The official `statutes.capitol.texas.gov` pages render via JavaScript and could not always be scraped verbatim; where that limited verbatim confirmation, it is flagged.

**The one idea that explains almost everything below:** In Texas the chain of title traces to one of four **"sovereigns of the soil" — Spain, Mexico, the Republic of Texas, or the State of Texas** — and (uniquely among the western states) the **State of Texas, not the U.S. federal government, was the source of original land grants.** Texas land was **never federal public domain**, so Texas has **no PLSS township-range grid**; its land is described by **metes-and-bounds keyed to an original GLO survey + abstract number.** Most "Texas vs. common law" differences flow from this.

# MODULE 1 — Historical & Common-Law Principles in Texas

## 1.1 Sources of Texas land titles (Spanish/Mexican grants, Republic, GLO patents)

**(a) Rule.** Every Texas title originates in a sovereign grant. Four sovereigns, in order:

| Sovereign | Era | Instrument |
|---|---|---|
| **Spain** | 1716–1821 | *Title* (porciones, town lots, livestock grants) |
| **Mexico** | 1821–1836 | *Title* (empresario/colonization grants; leagues/sitios + labores) |
| **Republic of Texas** | 1836–1845 | *Patent* / headright certificates |
| **State of Texas** | 1845–present | *Patent* |

A Spanish/Mexican "title" and a Republic/State "patent" are the instruments that **sever land from the sovereign**; all later deeds run from that first grantee. Perfected Spanish/Mexican titles remain valid today, and land already conveyed by them **cannot be re-patented** (sovereignty was already severed). The Republic distributed huge acreage via **headright** certificates (a first-class headright gave a head of family **one league and one labor, ~4,605.5 acres**, if resident before March 2, 1836).

**(b) Key citations.**
- Tex. Nat. Res. Code **Title 2 (Public Domain)**, **Ch. 51, Subch. F (Patents)** (issuance/requisites of state patents). ⚠️ Confirm the exact §§51.241–51.253 range before citing subsections.
- Tex. Nat. Res. Code **§31.001** (defines the "Land Office" = the GLO).
- **Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848)** (U.S. recognition of valid Mexican-era titles).
- Constitution of the Republic of Texas (1836) (honored valid Spain/Mexico grants).

> **Texas vs. common law:** In federal "public-land states," original title flows from the **United States** (federal GLO, Homestead Act, PLSS). In Texas it flows from the **State**; Texas land was **never federal public domain**, so there is **no township-range PLSS grid** — Texas grants are metes-and-bounds keyed to the original survey/grantee.

## 1.2 The Republic & retained public lands (1845 annexation)

**(a) Rule.** Texas was an **independent republic 1836–1845**. On annexation in 1845 it **retained all its vacant and unappropriated public lands** — the federal government did *not* take the public domain. Texas kept well over **200 million acres**, administered by the **General Land Office (GLO)**, and dedicated land revenue to the **Permanent School Fund** (later also the Permanent University Fund).

**(b) Key citations.**
- **Joint Resolution for Annexing Texas (Mar. 1, 1845), §2** — Texas "shall also retain all the vacant and unappropriated lands lying within its limits"; its debts were not to become a U.S. charge.
- **Texas Constitution of 1845** (carried the public domain into statehood; established the Permanent School Fund).

> **Texas vs. common law:** This is the single most consequential difference. Elsewhere the U.S. was the original grantor and the federal GLO/BLM administered disposal (much land still federally owned). In Texas the sovereign source is the **State** via the **GLO**; Texas has very little federal land, and the original grant in any Texas chain is a State (or pre-statehood) grant, never a federal patent.

## 1.3 The Texas General Land Office (GLO)

**(a) Rule.** Founded **December 22, 1836** by the First Congress of the Republic — the oldest existing Texas state agency. It maintains land records, produces official maps/surveys, and **issues patents**. Its archive holds roughly **35.5 million land-grant records (back to 1720)** and ~**80,000 maps**; the **Spanish Collection** is the depository for ~**4,200 Spanish/Mexican grants** (~26 million acres). South Texas Spanish/Mexican grants underwent a legislative **confirmation/validation** process (~1850–1852), after which the GLO issued confirming patents.

**(b) Key citations.** Tex. Nat. Res. Code **Ch. 31 (General Land Office)**, **§31.001** (definitions); GLO official history.

> **Texas vs. common law:** Most states have **no comparable state land office** holding sovereign-origin grant archives (their grants came from the federal government, held in BLM/National Archives records). The Texas GLO is both archival custodian of sovereign-era grants *and* an active administrator that still issues patents and manages state lands/minerals.

⚠️ Patent-conclusiveness / vacancy case law (Nat. Res. Code §§51.171–51.211) was not cleanly verifiable — pull directly before teaching that sub-topic. The Relinquishment Act (1919) mineral-classified-land cutoff date differs across sources — verify against statute.

## 1.4 Empresario system / colonization contracts (Mexican Texas)

**(a) Rule.** Under Mexican rule (1821–1836), **empresarios** contracted to settle a set number of families in exchange for land (roughly **5 leagues + 5 labores per 100 families**). Settlers had to convert to **Catholicism** and swear **allegiance to Mexico**. **Stephen F. Austin** inherited his father's 1821 contract (the **"Old Three Hundred"**) and ultimately settled 1,500+ families. About **forty-one empresario grants** were made 1821–1835.

**(b) Key citations.**
- **Mexican National Colonization Law of Aug. 18, 1824** (federal framework).
- **State Colonization Law of Coahuila y Tejas, March 1825** (basis of nearly all Texas contracts). ⚠️ Sources give a March 24 vs. 25, 1825 date — minor conflict.
- **Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848)** (protected valid Mexican-era titles).

> **Texas vs. common law:** The empresario system is a **civil-law (Spanish/Mexican)** colonization mechanism with **no English common-law equivalent** — land distributed by a state-licensed contractor under public-law conditions (religion, allegiance, residency), not private-market conveyance or the Anglo headright/homestead model. These grants survived the change in sovereignty, so many South/Central Texas chains bottom out in a Mexican-era league-and-labor grant in the GLO Spanish Collection.

## 1.5 Texas surveying units — vara, labor, league

**(a) Rules (conversions):**

| Unit | Definition | Acres | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Vara** | **33⅓ in (33.333…)** = 2.7778 ft | — | 36 varas = exactly 100 ft; 1,900.8 varas = 1 mile; ≈5,645.4 sq varas = 1 acre |
| **Labor** | 1,000,000 sq varas (1,000 varas square) | **~177.1** | farmland |
| **League / sitio (legua)** | 25,000,000 sq varas (5,000 varas square) | **~4,428.4** | grazing land; = 25 labors |
| **League + labor** | 26,000,000 sq varas | **~4,605.5** | standard married-head-of-family grant |

Grant sizing under the 1824/1825 colonization laws keyed to use: farming → **≥1 labor**; stock-raising → **1 league**; both → **1 labor + 1 league**. A single man received **1/3 league (~1,476 acres)**.

**(b) Authority.** The vara was **legally fixed at 33⅓ inches** by **Art. 5730, Revised Civil Statutes (Acts of 1919, eff. June 17, 1919)**. Tex. Nat. Res. Code **Ch. 21 (Surveys and Field Notes)** continues to govern public-land surveys; the GLO still uses varas in archived grant records and field notes.

> **Texas vs. common law (PLSS):** Texas kept its Spanish/Mexican system and **never adopted the federal Public Land Survey System**. Texas uses irregular **metes-and-bounds tracts** identified by **survey name + abstract number** (GLO), not the PLSS grid of chains/rods, 640-acre sections, and township/range. The two grids are mathematically unrelated — Texas descriptions cannot be read in township/range terms.

⚠️ Acre figures are rounded and depend on the 33⅓-in vara; **historical varas varied** (Spanish "Vara de Burgos" ~32.9 in; Mexican vara ~32.99 in), so original surveyed acreages differ marginally. Do not blindly apply a non-Texas vara value to a Texas grant. The Art. 5730 (1919) cite comes from secondary surveying references; the 33⅓-in standard itself is corroborated by TSHA/NIST.

## 1.6 Types of estates under Texas law

**(a) Rules.** Fee simple absolute (largest estate); **defeasible fees** — fee simple determinable ("so long as," "until"; leaves a **possibility of reverter**) and fee simple subject to condition subsequent ("but if," "provided that"; leaves a **right of re-entry/power of termination**); **life estate** (incl. *pur autre vie*; life tenant owes a no-waste duty); **remainder** (future interest in a third party); **reversion** (future interest retained by grantor). Texas **presumes a conveyance passes the entire estate (fee simple)** unless a lesser estate is expressly limited.

**(b) Key citations.**
- Tex. Prop. Code **§5.001** (fee simple passes "unless the estate is limited by express words or unless a lesser estate is conveyed").
- Tex. Prop. Code **§5.042 ("Abolition of Common-Law Rules,"** eff. Jan. 1, 1984): abolishes (1) the **Rule in Shelley's Case**, (2) the rule forbidding a remainder to the grantor's heirs, (3) the **doctrine of worthier title**, and (4) a homestead-lien rule — giving effect to such limitations "according to the intent of the conveyor."

> **Texas vs. common law:** Texas **abolished** the Rule in Shelley's Case and the Doctrine of Worthier Title (§5.042); a remainder "to A for life, then to A's heirs," or a remainder to the grantor's own heirs, is given effect per **grantor intent** rather than merged or recharacterized.

⚠️ **Correction to a common premise:** §5.042 does **NOT** abolish the **doctrine of destructibility of contingent remainders** — that doctrine is not listed in the statute. Texas has rejected destructibility as a matter of **case law** (honoring grantor intent), not via §5.042. Do not attribute destructibility to §5.042.

## 1.7 Types of deeds under Texas law

**(a) Rules.**
- **General warranty deed** — warrants title against **all** defects, back through the entire chain.
- **Special warranty deed** — warrants only against defects arising **"by, through, or under" the grantor** (recognized by drafting practice/case law).
- **Quitclaim deed** — conveys only **whatever interest, if any, the grantor has**; no warranty; **not a muniment of title**.

**(b) Key citations.**
- Tex. Prop. Code **§5.022 ("Form")** — statutory warranty-deed form ("grant, sold, and conveyed"; "**warrant and forever defend** … against every person whomsoever lawfully claiming"). Subsec. (b): a warranty is not required.
- Tex. Prop. Code **§5.023 ("Implied Covenants")** — "grant"/"convey" imply **only** (1) the grantor has not previously conveyed the estate to another, and (2) the estate is **free from encumbrances**.

> **Texas vs. common law:** At common law, "grant" implied a broad covenant of **seisin** (that the grantor actually owns the estate). **Texas §5.023 deliberately narrows** the implied covenant to no-prior-conveyance + no-encumbrances — it does **not** imply that the grantor actually owns the property. The general warranty in Texas comes from the express "warrant and forever defend" language of §5.022.

## 1.8 The Texas quitclaim peculiarity — grantee generally NOT a BFP

**(a) The classic rule.** A grantee under a **quitclaim** (and successors) is **generally NOT a bona fide purchaser**. The quitclaim is treated as **notice** that the grantor's title may be defective, so the grantee takes **subject to** prior outstanding claims even if value was paid without actual notice.

**(b) Key cases.**
- *Woodward v. Ortiz*, 237 S.W.2d 286, 291–92 (Tex. 1951) — quitclaim purchaser cannot enjoy BFP protection.
- *Houston Oil Co. of Texas v. Niles*, 255 S.W. 604, 610 (Tex. Comm'n App. 1923) — foundational; a quitclaim in the chain bars the innocent-purchaser defense.
- *Cook v. Smith*, 174 S.W. 1094 (Tex. 1915) — the "quitclaim" label is **not conclusive**; courts examine the **whole instrument** (does it convey the *property itself* — BFP possible — or only the grantor's *right, title, and interest* — no BFP?).
- *Geodyne Energy Income Prod. P'ship I-E v. Newton Corp.*, 161 S.W.3d 482 (Tex. 2005) — "A warranty deed to land conveys property; a quitclaim deed conveys the grantor's rights in that property, if any." Classification turns on the **granting language taken as a whole**, not the label.
- *Rogers v. Ricane Enters.*, 884 S.W.2d 763 (Tex. 1994) — a quitclaim "is not a conveyance or a muniment of title."

> **Texas vs. common law:** Texas is **comparatively strict** — a quitclaim grantee is presumptively on notice and generally cannot claim BFP status, whereas many states treat a quitclaim grantee like any other purchaser for BFP purposes.

⚠️ **Critical 2026 update — the rule has been statutorily softened.** Tex. Prop. Code **§13.006 ("Effect of Recording Quitclaim Deed,"** added 87th Leg., **eff. Sept. 1, 2021**): after the **fourth anniversary** of recording, a quitclaim deed (1) "**does not affect the question of the good faith** of a subsequent purchaser or creditor," and (2) "**is not notice** … of any unrecorded conveyance … or encumbrance." So for quitclaims **recorded on/after Sept. 1, 2021**, there is now a **four-year cleansing window** after which the quitclaim **no longer destroys BFP status** for downstream purchasers. The classic rule still applies to the **immediate** grantee and during the first four years. **Teach the two together.**

⚠️ Cite flags: *Diversified, Inc. v. Hall* = **23** S.W.3d 403 (Tex. App.—Houston [1st Dist.] 2000) — not "20"; spot-check the parallel *Cook v. Smith*, 107 Tex. 119 cite against the official reporter.

# MODULE 2 — Sources of Law & Presumptions

## 2.1 Sources of Texas law

**(a) Rules.** Hierarchy: (1) **Texas Constitution (1876**, current); (2) **statutes** (topical codes at statutes.capitol.texas.gov); (3) **administrative rules** (Texas Administrative Code); (4) **case law** (common law / stare decisis). Most relevant codes:
- **Texas Property Code** — estates, conveyances, recording, deeds, easements, restrictive covenants.
- **Texas Natural Resources Code** — public lands, the **GLO**, Permanent School Fund lands, surveys of state land (once a survey and its field notes are accepted by the GLO, they are legally presumed correct).
- **Texas Civil Practice & Remedies Code Ch. 16** — adverse possession (§§16.021–16.028) and the limitations period for **suits against surveyors** (§16.011). *(Common trap: adverse possession is NOT in the Property Code.)*
- **Texas Occupations Code Ch. 1071** (Professional Land Surveying Practices Act) and **Ch. 1001** (Engineering Practice Act) — surveyor/engineer licensing enabling acts.

**Administrative rules — TAC.** TBPELS (**Texas Board of Professional Engineers and Land Surveyors**, created by **HB 1523, 86th Leg. 2019**, merging the former engineering and surveying boards) rules are in **Texas Administrative Code, Title 22 (Examining Boards), Part 6**: **Ch. 134** (licensing), **Ch. 136** (firm registration), **Ch. 138** ("Compliance and Professionalism for Surveyors" — standards of practice/conduct). *(Older Chs. 661/663 were repealed and merged into 136/138 — use current chapters.)* The GLO also adopts rules under the Natural Resources Code.

> **Texas vs. common law:** Substance is conventional common law, but Texas is distinctive in (i) a **state-owned public-land regime under the GLO/Natural Resources Code** with no analog in most states, and (ii) the **2019 consolidation of engineers and surveyors into one board (TBPELS)** — many states keep separate surveying boards.

## 2.2 Texas court structure

**(a) Rules.** Trial courts: **District Courts** (general jurisdiction — **title/boundary disputes, trespass-to-try-title, quiet-title** start here; Tex. Const. art. V, §8); **County Courts** (constitutional + statutory "county courts at law"); **Justice (JP) Courts**; **Municipal Courts**. Intermediate appellate: the **Texas Courts of Appeals**. **Two co-equal highest courts**: the **Supreme Court of Texas** (final authority for **civil** and juvenile cases — boundary/title cases end here; 9 members) and the **Texas Court of Criminal Appeals** (final authority for **criminal** cases; 9 members). *(Tex. Const. art. V, §§1, 3, 5.)*

⚠️ **2026 update on the appellate count:** Texas historically had **14** Courts of Appeals. **As of 2024 there are effectively 15** — the 14 regional districts **plus a statewide Fifteenth Court of Appeals** (S.B. 1045, 88th Leg. 2023), which hears appeals from the new Texas **Business Court** and most civil appeals where **the State / a state agency / official is a party**. Its justices stand for first statewide election in **November 2026**. Ordinary private boundary appeals still go to the **regional** court of appeals; many older texts still say "14."

> **Texas vs. common law / typical structure:** Most U.S. states and the federal system have a **single** court of last resort hearing both civil and criminal appeals. Texas **splits this in two** — a design shared with **only one other state, Oklahoma**. Practical consequence for surveyors: because boundary/title disputes are **civil**, the apex authority is the **Supreme Court of Texas**; the Court of Criminal Appeals never touches boundary precedent.

## 2.3 Texas recording act — a NOTICE state

**(a) Rule.** Texas is a **pure "notice" jurisdiction**. A subsequent purchaser (i) for valuable consideration (ii) without notice (actual or constructive) of a prior unrecorded conveyance takes **free of it** — and **prevails even if the prior grantee records first, or the BFP never records at all**. Recording by the BFP is not a condition of winning.

**(b) Key statutes.**
- Tex. Prop. Code **§13.001 ("Validity of Unrecorded Instrument")** — a conveyance/mortgage/deed of trust is **"void as to a creditor or to a subsequent purchaser for a valuable consideration without notice"** unless acknowledged and filed for record; but **binding** on the parties, their heirs, and a purchaser who does not pay value **or who has notice**.
- Tex. Prop. Code **§13.002 ("Effect of Recording")** — a properly recorded instrument is **"notice to all persons"** (constructive notice).
- Tex. Prop. Code **§11.001 ("Place of Recording")** — record in the **county where the land lies**.

> **Texas vs. common law:** Common law was "first in time, first in right." Of the three statutory regimes — **race** (first to record wins, even with notice), **notice** (later BFP without notice wins, recording order irrelevant), **race-notice** (later BFP wins only if without notice **and** records first) — **Texas is pure notice.** The decisive distinction: a Texas BFP without notice **beats a prior unrecorded grantee even though the BFP records second (or never)** — the statute contains **no recording-priority requirement** for the BFP. (In a race-notice state like California, that same BFP would lose without winning the race to the courthouse.)

## 2.4 Bona fide purchaser doctrine in Texas

**(a) Elements.** (1) **Valuable consideration** (not nominal; a gift/quitclaim-only taker generally fails); (2) **good faith**; (3) **without notice** — neither **actual** nor **constructive**. Constructive notice arises from **the records** (§13.002) and from **possession** of the land.

**(b) Key case.** *Madison v. Gordon*, 39 S.W.3d 604 (Tex. 2001) — leading authority on **possession as constructive notice**: possession charges a purchaser with constructive notice **only if** it is **"visible, open, exclusive, and unequivocal."** A tenant's occupancy of one unit in a fourplex was not exclusive/unequivocal, so it imparted no constructive notice; judgment for the purchaser.

> **Texas vs. common law:** Elements are mainstream American doctrine, but Texas's **four-adjective possession test** ("visible, open, exclusive, and unequivocal") is a stricter, more defined formulation than the looser "open and notorious" standard used elsewhere — ambiguous/shared possession (tenants, multi-unit buildings) frequently **fails** to defeat BFP status in Texas. Combined with the **quitclaim wrinkle** (§1.8), Texas is comparatively protective of clean record purchasers.

⚠️ *Houston Oil Co. v. Hayden*, 104 Tex. 175, 135 S.W. 1149 (1911) is the older inquiry-notice authority, but the pinpoint page was not re-verified — confirm before quoting. *Madison v. Gordon* is the fully verified anchor.

## 2.5 Legal presumptions in Texas boundary law

**(a) Dignity/hierarchy of calls.** When calls conflict, higher dignity controls: **(1) natural monuments** > **(2) artificial monuments** > **(3) course (bearing)** > **(4) distance** > **(5) quantity/area**. A **rebuttable presumption** that yields to the **controlling intent** of the grant and the **footsteps of the original surveyor**. Foundational case: *Stafford v. King*, 30 Tex. 257 (1867) (full treatment in Modules 4 & 5).

**(b) Footsteps / intent.** The cardinal Texas principle: a retracing surveyor and the court must **follow the footsteps of the original surveyor** and give effect to the **grant's intent**, locating lines **where they were actually run**, even against later "more accurate" measurements.

**(c) Strip-and-gore, centerline, riparian.**
- **Strip-and-gore doctrine** — a grantor conveying land abutting a narrow strip/gore (abandoned road, thin remnant) is presumed **not to retain** it if it has no practical use apart from the conveyed tract, absent a clear reservation.
- **Centerline / center-of-road presumption** — land bounded "by/along" a road, street, or non-navigable watercourse is presumed to carry title **to the center** (subject to the public easement), unless clearly excluded.
- **Riparian — gradient boundary rule (distinctly Texas):** *Motl v. Boyd*, 116 Tex. 82, 286 S.W. 458 (Tex. 1926) (full treatment in Module 6). *State v. Balli*, 144 Tex. 195, 190 S.W.2d 71 (Tex. 1944) — leading authority on the dignity Texas courts give Spanish/Mexican original grant boundaries (Padre Island). ⚠️ 190 S.W.2d 71 is the Supreme Court opinion; a civil-appeals opinion exists at 173 S.W.2d 522 — specify which.

> **Texas vs. common law:** The dignity-of-calls hierarchy and footsteps doctrine are well-developed Texas common law (substance broadly shared). The **gradient boundary rule** is **distinctively Texas**; most states use the ordinary/mean high-water mark or thread of the stream rather than Texas's midway-gradient-of-the-cut-bank construction.

# MODULE 3 — Property-Law Doctrines in Texas

## 3.1 Easements

### Express easements
**Rule.** Created by a written instrument satisfying the Statute of Frauds (Tex. Bus. & Com. Code **§26.01** — a conveyance of an interest in land must be in writing and signed). Express grants/reservations are the most-favored method of easement creation in Texas.

> **Texas vs. common law:** No material divergence on the rule. The Texas practical point: because the courts *disfavor* the implied/prescriptive routes (below), Texas access rights are far more often documented **expressly** than created by operation of law.

### Implied easement by prior use ("quasi-easement")
**Elements.** (1) **Unity of ownership** before severance; (2) use **open and apparent** at severance; (3) use **continuous** (so the parties must have intended it to pass with the land); (4) use **reasonably necessary** — a *lower* bar than for necessity easements.

**Key case.** *Hamrick v. Ward*, 446 S.W.3d 377 (Tex. 2014).

> **Texas vs. common law (CRITICAL):** In *Hamrick v. Ward*, the Texas Supreme Court **restricted prior-use for roadway/access claims**: a party claiming an implied easement *for roadway access* to a **landlocked, previously unified parcel must proceed under easement-by-necessity, NOT prior use.** This narrows a doctrine unsettled in Texas for ~125 years. Under general common law a claimant could often elect prior use (easier "reasonably necessary" standard) for access; Texas pushes landlocked road access into the **stricter necessity framework.** Prior use still applies to *non-access* uses (utility lines, drainage) where it remains "reasonably necessary."

### Easement by necessity
**Elements (Texas requires all three).** (1) **Unity of ownership** of the dominant + servient estates **prior to severance** (grantor owned both as a single unit/tract); (2) access is a **present strict necessity, not a mere convenience**; (3) necessity **existed at the time of severance**. The easement **terminates when the necessity ends** (e.g., when alternative legal access becomes available) — "strict necessity continuing."

**Key cases.**
- *Koonce v. Brite Estate*, 663 S.W.2d 451 (Tex. 1984) — defines unity of ownership; the grantor must have owned dominant + servient "as a unit or single tract" (a common owner of two *separately held* tracts does not satisfy it).
- *Staley Family Partnership, Ltd. v. Stiles*, 483 S.W.3d 545 (Tex. 2016) — reaffirms the three elements; claimant must prove an easement at severance would have provided access to a public road.
- *Hamrick v. Ward*, 446 S.W.3d 377, 382 (Tex. 2014) — channels landlocked road-access claims into necessity.

> **Texas vs. common law:** Texas demands **strict necessity** (true landlock — no legal access), whereas many jurisdictions accept *"reasonable" necessity* for necessity easements. Texas also enforces the **unity-of-ownership element rigorously** (*Koonce*) and treats the easement as **terminable on cessation** of the necessity.

### Prescriptive easements — Texas DISFAVORS these
**Elements.** For the **10-year** limitations period (borrowed from adverse-possession §16.026): (1) **open and notorious**; (2) **continuous and uninterrupted**; (3) **adverse/hostile** (permissive use can *never* ripen); (4) **exclusive**; for the full statutory period.

**THE TEXAS "EXCLUSIVE USE" REQUIREMENT (most important point).** In Texas "exclusive" means use by the claimant and **NOT** by the landowner. **Joint or shared use defeats the claim.** Texas courts hold that **"joint use of a road, no matter how long, cannot ripen into a prescriptive easement,"** and there must be an **"unmistakable assertion of a claim of exclusive ownership."**

**Key case.** *Tran v. Macha*, 213 S.W.3d 913 (Tex. 2006) — neighbors shared a driveway under a mutual mistake; the Texas Supreme Court **reversed** the easement, holding mutual/joint use does **not** support an exclusive, hostile claim.

> **Texas vs. common law (CRITICAL):** **Texas does NOT favor prescriptive easements** and strictly scrutinizes every element. The decisive divergence is the **exclusivity requirement.** Under the general common law / Restatement view, an easement is *non-possessory*, so most jurisdictions do **not** require exclusivity for prescription — shared roadway use can still create one. **Texas is the opposite: shared/joint use is normally fatal.** Because most driveway/road-sharing fact patterns involve some owner use, Texas prescriptive claims rarely succeed; Texas funnels access disputes toward **necessity easements and express grants.**

## 3.2 Adverse possession — Texas statutory limitation scheme

Texas adverse possession is **entirely statutory**, in **Tex. Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code Ch. 16, Subch. B**. Each statute has **distinct** requirements; a claimant pleads under the period whose elements it can meet.

**Definitions — §16.021.**
- **"Adverse possession"** = "an actual and visible appropriation of real property, commenced and continued under a **claim of right** that is inconsistent with and is hostile to the claim of another person."
- **"Color of title"** = a consecutive chain of transfers to the possessor that is *not* a regular chain from the sovereign, or a regular chain with a missing/defective link.
- **"Title"** = a regular chain of transfers from or under the sovereignty of the soil.
- **"Peaceable possession"** = continuous possession not interrupted by an adverse suit to recover the property.

| Statute | Period | Core requirement |
|---|---|---|
| **§16.024** | **3 yr** | peaceable & adverse possession under **title or color of title** (strongest paper basis → shortest period) |
| **§16.025** | **5 yr** | **cultivate/use/enjoy** + **pay applicable taxes** + claim under a **duly registered deed** (excludes quitclaim, forged deeds). Tax payment is a *hard* requirement; a single missed year is fatal. |
| **§16.026** | **10 yr** | peaceable & adverse, **cultivating/using/enjoying**; no instrument/tax needed but **capped at 160 acres** unless (i) a larger area is **enclosed**, or (ii) possession is under a recorded deed/memorandum fixing the boundaries |
| **§16.027** | **25 yr** | runs **regardless of a legal disability** (minority, unsound mind) — absolute long-stop |
| **§16.028** | **25 yr** | under a **recorded instrument** purporting to convey, **even if void** on its face or in fact (excludes quitclaim/forged); extends to the whole area described |

> **Texas vs. common law (CRITICAL):**
> - **Wholly statutory, multi-track scheme.** Unlike the single open/notorious/hostile/continuous/exclusive common-law test, Texas offers **five distinct limitation periods (3/5/10/25/25)**, each with its own elements.
> - **Tax-payment requirement is distinctive.** The **§16.025 five-year track requires paying property taxes** — absent from traditional common law and a frequent claim-killer (a Western feature).
> - **Acreage cap.** The **§16.026 160-acre limitation** (absent enclosure or recorded deed) has no general common-law analog.
> - **Void-instrument track.** **§16.028** lets a *recorded but void* instrument support a 25-year claim — broader than common-law "color of title."
> - **Tacking** across successive possessors in privity is allowed, but the statutory prerequisites (taxes, registered deed, recorded instrument) are stricter and more formalized than common-law adverse possession.

## 3.3 Encroachments
**Rule.** An encroachment is an unauthorized physical intrusion of an improvement (fence, building, eave, driveway) onto a neighbor's land. Texas remedies: (a) **trespass to try title / quiet title** (Tex. Prop. Code Ch. 22), (b) **injunction** to remove, or (c) **damages**. A long-standing encroachment can mature into title via **adverse possession** (Ch. 16) or into a use-right via **prescriptive easement** — but both are hard in Texas (strict statutory elements; exclusivity defeated by joint use, *Tran v. Macha*).

> **Texas vs. common law:** Texas has **no special "innocent improver"/relative-hardship statute** that automatically lets a good-faith encroacher buy the strip; title passes only through the statutory adverse-possession tracks (courts may still apply equitable balancing for *injunctive* relief). The surveyor's measured boundary location is typically dispositive, and **acquiescence/agreed-boundary** doctrines are applied narrowly.

## 3.4 Dedication (statutory, common-law, implied) — and the 1981 limitation

**Statutory dedication.** Made by recording a **plat/map** marking streets/roads/areas for public use, under the platting statutes (Tex. Loc. Gov't Code Chs. 212 (municipal) / 232 (county); Tex. Prop. Code Ch. 12 plat recording). **Plat approval is NOT automatically acceptance** — some affirmative public acceptance is generally still needed.

**Common-law dedication.** Requires (1) the landowner's act(s) showing **donative intent**; (2) **public reliance**/benefit; (3) **offer and acceptance** (formal or informal — e.g., the authority grades/repairs the road, the public uses it, or the county stops taxing it).

**Implied (common-law) dedication & the 1981 statute.** Historically implied dedication arose from a landowner's course of conduct + public use, **no writing required** (the three elements plus an "additional factor" implying donative intent — permitting public grading/repair, selling parcels off a plat showing the road, an express public representation, or fencing the road off from the rest of the tract).

**THE 1981 STATUTE — Tex. Transp. Code Ch. 281** ("Acquisition of Public Interest in Private Road by Certain Counties"). Effective **August 1981**, the Legislature **abolished implied dedication as a means of establishing a public road on private property in less-populous counties** (Ch. 281 applies to **counties of 50,000 or fewer**). There, a public interest in a private road can be acquired **only** by **purchase, condemnation, an express written dedication, or a final judgment of adverse possession.** A dedication must be **(1) an explicit voluntary grant** of use for public purposes, **(2) communicated in writing to the county commissioners court.** **Oral / "overt act" / implied dedication is no longer sufficient** in covered counties. **Not retroactive** — roads impliedly dedicated **before August 1981** can still be established under the old doctrine. (Related: Transp. Code **Ch. 258** lets counties adopt a **county road map** to clarify/confirm existing public roads.)

**Key cases.** *Holly v. Newberry Ranches of Texas* (Tex. App.—San Antonio 2023) (applies these principles). ⚠️ *Las Vegas Pecan & Cattle Co. v. Zavala County*, 682 S.W.2d 254 (Tex. 1984) is the commonly-cited common-law dedication-elements case but was not independently re-verified — treat as a starting point.

> **Texas vs. common law (CRITICAL):** Under general common law, **implied dedication of public roads remains viable.** **Texas has partially abolished it:** in **counties of 50,000 or fewer (Transp. Code Ch. 281, since Aug. 1981)**, you **cannot** create a public road by implied/oral dedication — you need a **written dedication to the commissioners court**, purchase, condemnation, or an adverse-possession judgment. Major surveying-practice point: for a post-1981 road in a small county, public status cannot rest on "everyone has always used it."

# MODULES 4 & 5 — Conveyances & Boundary-Conflict Resolution in Texas

## 4.1 Order/priority of conflicting calls — "dignity of calls"

**(a) Rule.** When calls in a single description conflict, they are ranked by relative reliability ("dignity"), highest to lowest:

1. **Calls for natural objects/monuments** (rivers, creeks, lakes, mountains, rocks)
2. **Calls for artificial objects/monuments** (marked trees, stakes, mounds, set iron, fences)
3. **Calls for adjoinder** (an adjacent, already-established survey line/boundary) — modern Texas treatises insert this between artificial monuments and course
4. **Course** (bearing/direction)
5. **Distance**
6. **Quantity / area / acreage** (weakest)

**(b) Seminal case — *Stafford v. King*, 30 Tex. 257 (1867).** The foundational Texas authority. Verified language:

> "Natural objects are mountains, lakes, rivers, creeks, rocks, and the like. Artificial objects are marked lines, trees, stakes, etc."

> "Course and distance are the most unreliable calls; distance is less reliable than course … hence the general rule, that course and distance yield to natural objects … while, under certain circumstances, course and distance may control, yet generally they are but guides to the other calls."

*Stafford* summarized the controlling order as **"1, natural objects; 2, artificial objects; 3, course and distance."** (The intermediate "adjoinder" tier, the splitting of course from distance, and quantity at the bottom are refinements developed in later Texas case law/treatises; *Stafford* itself collapses the last tier into "course and distance.")

*Stafford* also anchors the **"footsteps"** rule:
> "The actual identification of the survey, the footsteps of the surveyor upon the ground, should always be followed, by whatever rule they may be traced."

⚠️ Pinpoint pages for the calls discussion fall in the **30 Tex. 257, ~270–272** range; "at 257" pins from secondary sources are approximate — confirm exact pinpoints in the reporter before quoting in print.

**(c) Texas nuance.** Texas fully adopts the hierarchy, but it is **only a rule of construction** — a set of reliability presumptions — that **always yields to the parties' intent and to the actual footsteps of the original surveyor.** *Stafford* frames the rule conditionally: adopt the construction "most consistent with the intention apparent upon the face of the patent, read in the light of the surrounding circumstances." A lower-dignity call (even quantity) can prevail when the instrument and circumstances show that was the intent; a found, identifiable monument on the ground controls over conflicting field-note calls.

> **Texas vs. common law:** Essentially identical ordering — natural → artificial → course → distance → quantity is the general American rule, and *Stafford v. King* is one of the most-cited 19th-century American statements of it (influential beyond Texas). The Texas emphasis is on *Stafford*'s twin caveats: (1) **intent controls** over the mechanical hierarchy, and (2) the **original surveyor's footsteps**, where ascertainable, override even the field-note calls.

## 4.2 Intent of the parties — the paramount rule of construction

**(a) Rule.** The controlling objective in construing a deed/grant is the **intent of the parties as expressed within the four corners** of the instrument, read in light of surrounding circumstances. The dignity-of-calls hierarchy is a *subordinate* tool to *find* that intent when language conflicts.

**(b) Key cases.**
- *Stafford v. King*, 30 Tex. 257 (1867) — construction must be "most consistent with the intention apparent upon the face of the patent."
- *Bass v. Harper*, 441 S.W.2d 825 (Tex. 1969) — modern deed-construction focuses on intent expressed in the deed and on harmonizing all parts.
- *Concho Resources, Inc. v. Ellison*, No. 19-0233 (Tex. 2021) — modern boundary/deed-conflict decision emphasizing intent and that a specific (metes-and-bounds) description controls over a general one when they cannot be harmonized.
- ⚠️ *Luckel v. White*, 819 S.W.2d 459 (Tex. 1991); *Wenske v. Ealy*, 521 S.W.3d 791 (Tex. 2017) — cited from general "four-corners/harmonize" doctrine; verify pinpoints before quoting.

**(c) Texas treatment.** Intent is **paramount**; priority-of-calls rules are merely aids. When the priority of calls *cannot* resolve a conflict, the parties' intention controls, and in a boundary suit the location of the line is ultimately a **question of fact** for the factfinder.

> **Texas vs. common law:** Same fundamental principle — intent governs everywhere. Texas's modern caselaw (*Bass v. Harper* forward) sharpened the "four-corners / harmonize the whole instrument" method and downgraded mechanical default canons.

## 4.3 Senior / junior rights doctrine

**(a) Rule.** When a grantor conveys parcels **sequentially**, the earlier (**senior**) conveyance takes the full measure of what its description grants; the later (**junior**) conveyance takes only the **remainder**. A grantor cannot convey land already conveyed to a senior grantee. Seniority does **not** expand a grant beyond its own description — it only resolves overlaps and gaps in the senior's favor.

**(b) Authority.** A common-law retracement doctrine rather than a single Texas statute, reinforced by the recording/title-priority framework and by **22 Tex. Admin. Code Ch. 138** (TBPELS rules direct surveyors to respect junior/senior rights and the record chain in retracement). ⚠️ Confirm the exact section (≈ §138.85, Subch. E) before citing.

**(c) Texas treatment.** Standard senior-rights application: the senior deed/patent "gets the full measure of what is described" when it conflicts with a junior deed; the junior is cut back to the residue. This dovetails with the Texas patent system (GLO patents create the senior title against which later subdivisions are junior).

> **Texas vs. common law:** Uniform across states; no notable Texas departure. The Texas-specific overlay is **regulatory** — TBPELS rules affirmatively *require* surveyors to honor junior/senior rights and the record chain.

## 4.4 Sequential vs. simultaneous conveyances

**(a) Rule.** The discrepancy-resolution method depends on how parcels were created:
- **Sequential** (carved out one after another over time): **senior/junior rights govern.** The senior takes its full description; the junior absorbs any excess or deficiency. Excess/deficiency is **not** prorated between senior and junior.
- **Simultaneous** (multiple parcels created at one instant from a common scheme — classically, lots in a recorded subdivision plat): **no lot is senior.** Excess/deficiency is **apportioned proportionately** among the affected lots (see 4.5). The order in which individual lots later *sold* is **immaterial** to seniority among them.

**(b) Texas treatment.** The recorded-subdivision plat is the paradigm simultaneous conveyance; metes-and-bounds carve-outs from a larger tract over time are the paradigm sequential conveyance.

> **Texas vs. common law:** Identical framework — the sequential/simultaneous dichotomy and its consequences (senior rights vs. apportionment) are general American boundary-law doctrine applied without material variation in Texas.

## 4.5 Proportionate measurement / apportionment of excess & deficiency

**(a) Rule.** When measured ground distances between controlling, **undisputed monuments** differ from record (plat/deed) distances, the excess/shortage found *within* a simultaneously-created block is distributed among interior subdivisions **in proportion to their record lengths** — no single lot absorbs the whole error. The PLSS/BLM technical formulations:
- **Single proportionate measurement** ("two-way"): restores a lost corner on a **single line** between two found monuments; the corrected interval bears the same ratio to the corrected whole as the record interval bears to the record whole.
- **Double proportionate measurement**: restores a lost corner **controlled in two directions** (common to four sections; established from four surrounding found monuments), proportioning independently in both directions.

**When apportionment applies vs. senior rights:**
- **Apportionment** applies to **simultaneous conveyances** (original subdivision/plat lots of equal dignity) and to restoring **lost** original PLSS corners.
- **Senior rights (no apportionment)** apply to **sequential conveyances**: make the senior whole first; the junior takes the excess or eats the deficiency.
- Proportioning is appropriate only for **ordinary distributed measurement error**, not an isolable **gross blunder** (which is placed where it belongs).
- Apportionment is a **last resort** — **found monuments / original corners control first**; proportion only where the corner is genuinely **lost** (not merely **obliterated** and recoverable from collateral evidence).

**(b) Authorities.** The single-/double-proportion methodology is codified in the federal **BLM *Manual of Surveying Instructions*** — the national standard for restoring lost PLSS corners. ⚠️ **Honest gap:** there is **no controlling Texas Supreme Court opinion adopting "single/double proportionate measurement" by name.** In Texas this is treated as established surveying/common-law practice (incorporated through expert testimony and retracement rules), not a marquee Texas case. Some commentators (e.g., Jeff Lucas) argue the "must prorate" instinct is over-applied and that found monuments/due process should override blind proportioning — a caution worth teaching, not a Texas holding. **Do not attribute a famous Texas case to "double proportionate measurement."**

**(c) Texas treatment.** Texas accepts apportionment for **simultaneously-created** lots of equal dignity and rejects it where senior rights govern. Strong Texas overlay: because Texas land is largely held under **GLO patents/original surveys** (not a uniform federal PLSS township grid), much Texas retracement turns on locating the **original surveyor's footsteps and monuments** rather than mechanical PLSS proportioning. **Proportioning is genuinely a fallback in Texas.**

> **Texas vs. common law / PLSS:** The single-/double-proportion *methods* are federal-PLSS/BLM constructs, not Texas inventions. Texas borrows the underlying common-law apportionment principle for simultaneous subdivisions but does **not** sit on the federal township grid for most of its land (it retained its public lands at annexation). So Texas leans heavily on **original-survey/footsteps** retracement and uses proportioning sparingly.

## 4.6 GLO survey rules & Texas resurvey ("footsteps") doctrine

**(a) Rule.** **Resurvey = retracement.** A resurvey does not create or relocate boundaries; it re-establishes the lines/corners of the **original** survey. The **footsteps of the original surveyor**, where ascertainable, **control** — even over inconsistent field-note calls. The dignity-of-calls hierarchy is invoked only when those footsteps *cannot* be retraced.

**(b) Key cases.**
- *Stafford v. King*, 30 Tex. 257 (1867) — Texas origin of the footsteps doctrine.
- *Silver Oil & Gas, Inc. v. EOG Resources, Inc.*, 246 S.W.3d 197, 204 (Tex. App.—San Antonio 2007, no pet.) — "the cardinal rule is that the footsteps of the original surveyor, if they can be ascertained, should be followed"; actual lines/corners on the ground control even if inconsistent with the field notes; absent ascertainable footsteps, prioritize natural → artificial → course → distance.
- *TH Investments, Inc. v. Kirby Inland Marine, L.P.*, 218 S.W.3d 173, 203 (Tex. App.—Houston [14th Dist.] 2007, pet. denied) — location of a survey line is a question of fact.
- *B & P Development, LLC v. Knighthawk, LLC*, No. 04-15-00575-CV, 2017 WL 1161227 (Tex. App.—San Antonio 2017, pet. denied) — competing-survey disputes.
- **GLO** administers original land-grant/patent surveys and records; **TBPELS** regulates surveyor conduct (**22 Tex. Admin. Code Ch. 138**), including retracement standards and respect for junior/senior rights and the title record.

**(c) Texas treatment.** Texas is a strong "footsteps" jurisdiction traceable directly to *Stafford v. King*. Because Texas titles flow from **original GLO surveys/patents** (Republic- and State-era grants) rather than a uniform federal PLSS grid, retracing the **original surveyor's actual work** is the dominant Texas methodology, with dignity-of-calls and proportioning as fallbacks.

> **Texas vs. common law / PLSS:** The footsteps/retracement principle is general American boundary law, but it has special force in Texas because of the GLO original-survey patent system. Unlike PLSS states (federal township-and-range grid; BLM Manual governs lost-corner restoration), Texas retained and surveyed its own public domain, so Texas resurvey law centers on the **original Texas surveyor's monuments and footsteps** administered through the GLO records, with PLSS-style proportioning used only as a last resort.

### Quick reference — dignity of calls (Texas, after *Stafford* + modern refinement)
1. Natural monuments/objects → 2. Artificial monuments/objects → 3. Adjoinder → 4. Course (bearing) → 5. Distance → 6. Quantity/area.
**All subordinate to:** (i) the **intent of the parties** (paramount) and (ii) the **footsteps of the original surveyor** where identifiable on the ground.

# MODULE 6 — Water Rights & Water Boundaries in Texas (HIGH PRIORITY)

> **Orientation.** Texas is genuinely unique on water boundaries. Two features have no parallel elsewhere: (1) the **gradient boundary method** for the margin of a navigable stream, and (2) the **dual coastal rule** under which **Spanish/Mexican grants use mean *higher* high water** while Anglo/state grants use mean high tide. Texas also retains an absolute **rule of capture** for groundwater and treats surface water as **State-owned**. Always check the patent's **date and sovereign source** before choosing a boundary rule.

## 6.1 Riparian vs. littoral rights
**(a) Rule.** "Riparian" rights attach to land bordering a **flowing watercourse** (river/stream); "littoral" rights attach to land bordering a **static body** (lake, sea, Gulf). For **non-navigable** streams the riparian owner's title runs to the **center thread** of the channel; for **navigable** streams private title stops at the bank (gradient boundary) and the **State owns the bed**. Littoral owners take to the relevant water line (high-water mark / high tide line), with the bed below typically in the State on navigable/tidal waters.

**(b) Authority.** Descriptive rather than codified; recognized throughout Texas water law alongside the navigability rules of Tex. Nat. Res. Code Ch. 21 and the surface-water provisions of the Water Code.

> **Texas vs. common law:** Texas's divergence is *substantive*, not terminological: (i) Texas treats **surface water as State-owned** and channels riparian *use* through the appropriation/permit system (6.9), so a Texas "riparian right" is far narrower than the classic eastern reasonable-use right; and (ii) the **boundary** of a navigable Texas stream is the **gradient boundary**, not the ordinary high-water mark used in many states (6.2).

## 6.2 The Texas gradient boundary method (THE defining peculiarity)
**(a) Rule.** The landward limit of the bed of a **navigable stream** — the line dividing State-owned bed from private upland — is the **gradient boundary**: a line along the bank **midway between (i) the lower level of the flowing water that just reaches the cut bank, and (ii) the higher level that just does not overtop the cut bank.** It is a *gradient* (a graded line following the bank slope) tied to the **"cut bank" / "qualified bank."** Refined as the **"mid-height point of the lowest 'acceptable'/qualified bank in the vicinity"** (*Brainard*).

**(b) Key cases.**
- *Oklahoma v. Texas*, 260 U.S. 606 (1923) — U.S. Supreme Court adopted the gradient boundary for the Texas–Oklahoma line on the Red River; its commissioner/surveyor **Arthur A. Stiles** developed the method ("water washed and relatively permanent elevation … at the outer line of the river bed").
- *Motl v. Boyd*, 116 Tex. 82, 286 S.W. 458 (1926) — Texas Supreme Court adopted the gradient boundary between public and private ownership along navigable streams; defined the **"bed"** as the soil "alternately covered and left bare … adequate to contain [the water] at its average and mean stage during an entire year, without reference to the extra freshets … or the extreme droughts."
- *Diversion Lake Club v. Heath*, 126 Tex. 129, 86 S.W.2d 441 (1935) — endorsed the gradient boundary.
- *Brainard v. State*, 12 S.W.3d 6 (Tex. 2000) — leading modern application (Canadian River); restated/refined the location method.
- A.A. Stiles, *The Gradient Boundary — The Line Between Texas and Oklahoma Along the Red River*, 30 Tex. L. Rev. (1950).

> **Texas vs. common law:** Virtually every other state fixes the riparian boundary at the **ordinary high-water mark (OHWM)** — an observable vegetation/scour line. Texas instead uses a *computed gradient line* at the **mid-height of the qualified bank**, requiring specialized survey work (water-level observation, bank profiling, gradient interpolation between qualified banks). Unique to Texas, and the single most distinctive concept in this module. *(It is a difficult expert survey; do not equate it with a simple high-water line.)*

## 6.3 Statutory definition of "navigable stream": the 30-foot rule
**(a) Rule.** A stream is **navigable in fact** (and thus public, with a State-owned bed) if it **"retains an average width of 30 feet from its mouth up."** The 30-foot measurement is of the **entire bed** (between banks), not the water flowing on any given day — so a sometimes-dry channel can still be navigable. (Texas also recognizes navigability-in-law where a stream is actually usable for public passage.)

**(b) Citation.** **Tex. Nat. Res. Code §21.001(3)**: "'Navigable stream' means a stream which retains an average width of 30 feet from the mouth up." Originates in an **1837 act of the Republic of Texas** (instructing surveyors not to cross such streams), carried into **Revised Statutes art. 5302** and recodified into §21.001. ⚠️ The modern §21.001(3) cite and 1837 origin are well-corroborated; the precise pre-codification article (commonly **Rev. Stat. art. 5302**) should be pulled directly if quoted verbatim.

> **Texas vs. common law:** The federal/common-law test of navigability turns on **navigability-in-fact for commerce** (*The Daniel Ball*). Texas *supplements* that with a **bright-line geometric test** — 30 feet of average bed width — found in essentially no other state. A small, non-commercial Texas creek can be legally "navigable," and therefore public with a State-owned bed, purely because of its average width.

## 6.4 The "Small Bill" of 1929
**(a) Rule/effect.** The 1929 "Small Bill" **relinquished and quitclaimed the State's interest in the *beds*** of certain streams to the patentees/adjoining riparian grantees, curing title clouds where earlier surveys/patents had crossed streambeds. Critically, it **reserved minerals to the State** (and retained sand/gravel), and **expressly preserved the public's and State's rights in the *waters***. So along a navigable stream the adjoining owner may hold (and pay taxes on) the bed, **but the public still retains navigation/recreation rights in the water, and the State keeps the minerals.**

**(b) Citation.** Codified historically as **Art. 5414a, Vernon's Revised Civil Statutes (Acts 1929)**. It validated patents including streambeds and let owners with insufficient upland make up acreage from the bed, while reserving minerals/sand/gravel to the State and protecting pre-existing express mineral reservations.

> **Texas vs. common law:** A Texas-specific *curative statute* born of the 1920s oil boom (the same Red River/Canadian River disputes that produced the gradient-boundary cases). Its hallmark — **conveying the bed's surface to private owners while severing and reserving the minerals to the State** — has no general common-law analog and still controls streambed mineral ownership in Texas title work.

## 6.5 Ownership of beds of navigable streams
**(a) Rule.** **The State of Texas owns the beds of navigable streams**, holding them **in trust for the public**. Private patents abutting a navigable stream run only to the **gradient boundary** (6.2); the bed below is sovereign land — subject to the Small Bill's partial relinquishment of the *bed surface* (but not minerals). For **non-navigable** streams the abutting owner takes title to the **center** of the channel.

**(b) Authority.** *Motl v. Boyd*, 116 Tex. 82, 286 S.W. 458 (1926); *Brainard v. State*, 12 S.W.3d 6 (Tex. 2000); Tex. Nat. Res. Code Ch. 21.

> **Texas vs. common law:** The State-owns-the-bed principle parallels the general American public-trust doctrine, **but Texas applies it through its own navigability tests** (the 30-foot statutory width and gradient-boundary line) rather than the federal commerce-based standard, so the *set* of streams whose beds the State owns is defined more broadly and differently.

## 6.6 Tidal/coastal boundaries: the dual standard (*Luttes v. State*)
**(a) Rule.** The seaward boundary of upland (dividing private upland from State-owned tidal seabed) depends on the **sovereign source of the grant**:
- **Spanish/Mexican grants** → bounded by the **line of mean *higher* high water (MHHW)** — the average of the *higher* of the two daily high tides over an ~18.6-year tidal epoch. This pushes the boundary *landward* (the State's seabed is larger).
- **Anglo-American / Republic- and State-of-Texas grants** → bounded by the **line of mean high tide / mean high water (the ordinary high-water mark)** — the average of *all* high tides over the epoch.

The difference exists because Spanish-Mexican civil law (in force when those grants issued) defined the "shore" by the reach of the *highest* (winter) tides, which the Court translated into the surveyable MHHW datum.

**(b) Key case.** *Luttes v. State*, 159 Tex. 500, 324 S.W.2d 167 (1959) — concerning the 1829 Mexican "Potrero de Buena Vista" grant on the Laguna Madre. The seaward boundary of a **Spanish/Mexican** grant is **mean higher high water**, distinguished from the **mean high tide** line for grants under Anglo-American (Texas) law. Controlling Texas authority on coastal upland boundaries.

> **Texas vs. common law:** The Anglo-American rule (*Borax Consol. v. City of Los Angeles*, 296 U.S. 10 (1935)) uses **mean high tide (MHW)** universally. Texas is **dual** — MHW for its own-law grants, but the **landward MHHW line for civil-law (Spanish/Mexican) grants** — a direct consequence of Texas's three-sovereign history. A coastal surveyor must first establish the chain of title back to the original sovereign before choosing the tidal datum.

## 6.7 The Texas Open Beaches Act (Nat. Res. Code Ch. 61) & *Severance v. Patterson*
**(a) Rule.** The **Texas Open Beaches Act (TOBA)** (originally 1959; **Tex. Nat. Res. Code Ch. 61**) guarantees the public a **free and unrestricted right of ingress/egress and use of Gulf-front beaches**, recognizing a public easement on the **dry beach between the line of mean low tide and the line of vegetation** where a prescriptive, dedicated, or customary easement exists. It creates a **prima facie presumption** that an area between mean low tide and the vegetation line is subject to a public easement. The easement historically **"rolls"** landward as the vegetation line migrates.

**(b) Key case.** *Severance v. Patterson*, 370 S.W.3d 705 (Tex. 2012) — the Texas Supreme Court **limited the rolling-easement doctrine**: a public beach easement may *gradually* migrate with naturally shifting beaches, **but it does NOT automatically "roll" onto previously private dry land after an *avulsive* event** (e.g., a hurricane suddenly moving the vegetation line landward). After avulsion, the State must establish a *new* easement rather than rely on the prior one rolling inland.

> **Texas vs. common law:** TOBA's statutory public-beach easement to the vegetation line is distinctively robust. The Texas wrinkle is the **gradual/avulsive distinction applied to the easement itself**: *Severance* imported the accretion/avulsion boundary principle (6.8) into beach-access law, so that **sudden** shoreline change *fixes* (rather than rolls) the public easement — narrowing what many assumed was an automatically migrating public right.

## 6.8 Accretion / reliction / avulsion / erosion
**(a) Rule.** Texas follows the general doctrine; the controlling variable is **gradual vs. sudden**:
- **Accretion** (gradual addition of alluvion) and **reliction** (gradual uncovering by receding water): the **boundary moves** — the owner gains land.
- **Erosion** (gradual loss): boundary moves landward; owner loses the eroded land.
- **Avulsion** (sudden, perceptible change — a flood cutting a new channel, a hurricane): the **boundary is FIXED** at its former location; it does **not** follow the new water line. Title does not shift merely because the water suddenly moved.

**(b) Authority.** General Texas boundary law; applied to streams via the gradient-boundary cases (*Motl v. Boyd*; *Brainard v. State*, 12 S.W.3d 6 (Tex. 2000)) and to the coast via *Severance v. Patterson*, 370 S.W.3d 705 (Tex. 2012).

> **Texas vs. common law:** The gradual-moves / avulsive-fixes framework is mainstream American law, so here Texas largely *aligns*. The distinctiveness is in the *boundary being measured* — the **gradient boundary** on streams (so accretion/erosion shift a computed gradient line, not a simple OHWM) and the **public-easement consequence** of avulsion on the Gulf coast under *Severance*.

## 6.9 Water use: surface water (State-owned, appropriation) vs. groundwater (rule of capture)

### Surface water
**(a) Rule.** **Surface water in Texas is owned by the State** (every river, natural stream, lake, bay, arm of the Gulf). Private parties acquire the right to *use* it only by **appropriation under a State-issued permit**, administered by the **Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ)**, on a **prior-appropriation ("first in time, first in right")** basis. Limited **domestic and livestock use** by streamside landowners survives without a permit.

**(b) Statutes/history.** **Tex. Water Code Ch. 11** (esp. **§11.021**: State ownership of surface water). Texas historically ran a **riparian/appropriation hybrid**, which produced decades of conflicting claims; the **Water Rights Adjudication Act of 1967** resolved this by **merging recognized riparian claims into the prior-appropriation permit system** (claimants had to file by the deadline or have their rights folded into the unified permit framework).

> **Texas vs. common law:** Eastern states use pure **riparian** surface-water law; western states use **prior appropriation**. Texas is a **historical hybrid statutorily *unified* into appropriation** in 1967 — an unusual posture. Bedrock point for surveyors/landowners: **owning the streambank does not give you a right to use the water** beyond domestic/livestock use; that requires a State permit.

### Groundwater
**(a) Rule.** Groundwater follows the **rule of capture** (English / "absolute ownership" rule): a landowner may pump and capture the water beneath his land — even to a neighbor's detriment — generally **without liability**. The landowner **owns the groundwater *in place*** as a vested, constitutionally protected property right (like oil and gas), subject to reasonable regulation by **Groundwater Conservation Districts (GCDs)**; over-regulation can be a **compensable taking**.

**(b) Key cases.**
- *Houston & Texas Central Railway Co. v. East*, 98 Tex. 146, 81 S.W. 279 (1904) — adopted the rule of capture; no liability for draining a neighbor's well/spring through pumping.
- *Edwards Aquifer Authority v. Day*, 369 S.W.3d 814 (Tex. 2012) — a landowner **owns the groundwater *in place*** as "an exclusive and private property right … of which he may not be deprived without a taking," protected by **Tex. Const. art. I, §17**; GCDs may regulate, but **excessive pumping limits can be a constitutional taking** requiring compensation.
- Institutional framework: the **Edwards Aquifer Authority** and the GCD network; **Tex. Water Code Ch. 36** (GCDs).

> **Texas vs. common law:** Most states abandoned absolute capture for **reasonable use, correlative rights, or the Restatement rule**. Texas is a notable holdout that **retains the rule of capture** while *layering* on **ownership-in-place** (*Day*). The result is distinctly Texan: near-unlimited common-law pumping rights, a recognized *vested ownership* in the water in place, and regulation only through GCDs that risks **takings liability** if it cuts too deeply.

# MODULE 7 — Metes & Bounds Descriptions in Texas

> ⚠️ **Framing note for instructors.** The old **22 TAC Chapter 663** surveying standards were **REPEALED**. After **HB 1523 (86th Leg., 2019)** merged the surveying board into **TBPELS**, the standards were re-codified (eff. 2021) into **22 TAC Chapter 138, "Compliance and Professionalism for Surveyors."** The **Professional and Technical Standards are now in 22 TAC Ch. 138, Subchapter E (§§138.81 et seq.)**, not §663. Material citing §663 is **outdated for 2026.**

## 7.1 Required elements of a metes-and-bounds description
**(a) Standard.** A metes-and-bounds description must unambiguously identify a single **closed** parcel: start at a defined point, run a continuous series of measured calls (bearing + distance) around the perimeter, tie to monuments, and return to the point of beginning. Governing rule: **22 TAC §138.91** ("Survey Drawing/Written Description/Report"): "Every description … shall provide a definite and unambiguous identification of the location of such boundaries and shall describe all monuments found or placed" (§138.91(b)); "All reports shall delineate the relationship between record monuments and the location of the boundaries surveyed" (§138.91(a)); "Courses shall be referenced … to an identifiable and monumented line or an established geodetic system" (§138.91(c)).

Required elements:
- **Caption / heading.** County, the **original GLO survey/abstract** (e.g., "the John Doe Survey, Abstract No. 123"), commonly the original grantee/league, plus acreage and a record reference to the parent tract. Texas captions almost always recite the abstract number because Texas land is organized by original land-grant surveys, not the PLSS grid.
- **Point of Beginning (POB).** Origin of the perimeter run. Under §138.87 (Monumentation), established by a monument at the point or by reference to a found monument. By Texas convention the POB is tied back to a **corner of the original GLO survey/abstract** so the parcel can be re-located independently of the surveyor's own work.
- **Bearings & distances (calls).** Each course states direction + length (e.g., "N 12°30' E, 210.50 ft"). Bearings tied to a stated **basis of bearing** (§138.91(c) — an identifiable monumented line or an established geodetic system such as the Texas State Plane Coordinate System / a NAD83 grid datum).
- **Monuments (natural & artificial).** Natural = rivers, creeks, established trees, ridgelines. Artificial = iron rods/pipes, concrete monuments, fences, road ROW lines. §138.91(e) requires stating **which monuments were found and which were placed**, plus "other monuments of record dignity relied upon." Monuments **control over** bearings/distances in a conflict.
- **Area / quantity.** Acreage (or sq ft; historically varas/labors/leagues). Texas treats stated area as the **least reliable** element — yields to monuments, then courses/distances; a check, not a control.
- **CLOSURE.** Calls must mathematically return to the POB, forming a closed figure with no gap/overlap; a description that does not close is legally defective. For Category 1A surveys a **closure report** is submitted separately; traverse/closure tolerances are set in **22 TAC §138.83** ("Precision and Accuracy") and the TSPS Manual of Practice.

> **Texas conventions / vs. common law:** Common-law metes-and-bounds (used in the 13 original colonies and Texas) describes a perimeter, unlike the federal **PLSS** rectangular system (township/range/section) used in most western states. Texas never adopted the PLSS; it retained the Spanish/Mexican grant framework, so every Texas description is anchored to an **original survey + abstract number**. The Texas dignity-of-calls hierarchy follows general common-law principles but is applied against the **GLO original-survey record**, not a sectional grid.

## 7.2 Texas conventions: tie to the original GLO survey/abstract & basis of bearing
**(a) Standard.** Texas descriptions are located within, and tied to, the **original land-grant survey**. The caption references the grant by grantee and abstract number (e.g., "situated in the **H. & T.C.R.R. Co. Survey, Abstract No. ___**, [County] County, Texas"). The POB and at least one controlling corner tie to a corner of that original survey. Bearings reference a stated **basis of bearing** (§138.91(c)).

**(b) Authority.** 22 TAC §138.91(a)–(c), (e); the survey-category/accuracy framework in 22 TAC §138.81 et seq. (Subch. E). The original-survey/abstract framework derives from the GLO land-grant system (Occupations Code §1071.002 defines "state land surveying" as including "determining by survey the location or relocation of original land grant boundaries and corners").

**(c) Conventions.** The abstract number is the spine of Texas land identification. The basis of bearing today is typically grid (Texas State Plane Coordinate System, NAD83, by zone); historic descriptions used magnetic/assumed bearings — a known source of rotation discrepancies in retracements.

> **Texas conventions / vs. common law:** In PLSS states the locational anchor is the section/township/range corner set by the federal GLO/BLM rectangular survey. In Texas the anchor is the **original land-grant survey + abstract**, administered by the **Texas General Land Office** (a state, not federal, body) — the single biggest structural difference.

## 7.3 Vara-based historical descriptions
**(a) Standard.** Many original Texas field notes are in **varas**. Texas law fixed the vara at **exactly 33⅓ inches** — **Art. 5730, Revised Civil Statutes (Acts of 1919, eff. June 17, 1919)**.

**(b) Conversions (verify each time).**
- 1 vara = 33⅓ in = **2.7778 ft** (33.333… ÷ 12).
- Vara → feet: × 2.7778 (equivalently, **÷ 0.36**). Feet → vara: × **0.36**.
- Area: **1 labor = 1,000,000 sq varas ≈ 177.1 ac**; **1 league (sitio) = 25 labors ≈ 4,428 ac** (≈ 6.9 sq mi).
- Example: "**S 45° E, 950 vrs**" = 950 × 2.7778 ≈ **2,638.9 ft**.

**(c) Conventions.** Older calls abbreviate varas as "vrs." A retracing surveyor must convert varas to feet and reconcile the historic (often magnetic/assumed) bearing basis to a modern datum. The vara persists in GLO field notes and older recorded deeds.

> **Texas conventions / vs. common law:** The vara is essentially unique to Texas (and former Spanish-grant areas of the Southwest). Its value varied historically (California ~33 in, Mexico ~32.99 in), but **Texas statutorily uses 33⅓ in** — do not blindly apply a foreign vara value to a Texas grant.

## 7.4 Role of the General Land Office (GLO)
**(a) Standard.** The **GLO** is custodian of the State's original land-grant records — patents, field notes, sketches, maps — dating to the Spanish/Mexican period. A surveyor retracing an original survey must consult the GLO record to locate **original grant boundaries and corners**, then follow the original surveyor's footsteps on the ground.

**(b) Facts/citations.** The GLO Archives hold ~**35.5 million land-grant records (back to 1720)** and ~**80,000 maps** (back to 1753); the Sketch Files include surveyor reports and field notes. **Patents** sever land from sovereignty (final step of a grant). Statutory link: **Occupations Code §1071.002** — a **Licensed State Land Surveyor (LSLS)** is licensed "to survey land in which the state or the permanent school fund has an interest," and "state land surveying" includes "determining by survey the location or relocation of original land grant boundaries and corners" and "filing field notes in the General Land Office."

**(c) Conventions.** Original surveys of state land, and corrections to original grant boundaries, require an **LSLS** (a higher GLO-issued credential than RPLS). Retracement of GLO originals follows the "footsteps" doctrine and dignity-of-calls.

> **Texas conventions / vs. common law:** In PLSS states the federal BLM (successor to the federal General Land Office) keeps the rectangular survey and federal corner records. Texas, having entered the Union retaining its public lands, runs its **own state GLO** with its own grant archive — a fundamental jurisdictional difference.

## 7.5 TBPELS rules, standards of practice, Category 1A, monuments, RPLS licensure
**(a) Standards.**
- **Governing statute:** **Occupations Code Chapter 1071** — the **Professional Land Surveying Practices Act** (per §1071.001). ⚠️ Multiple sources confirm Ch. 1071 "may be cited as the Professional Land Surveying Practices Act," but the exact §1071.001 short-title sentence was not pulled verbatim — verify before quoting. Definitions: **§1071.002** ("professional surveying," "registered professional land surveyor," "licensed state land surveyor," "state land surveying"). Licensure: **§1071.251** (license required), **§1071.252** (application), **§1071.254** (RPLS qualifications), **§1071.255** (LSLS qualifications). Discipline: **§§1071.401–1071.405**.
- **Administrative rules (current, 2026):** **22 TAC Part 6, Chapter 138** — "Compliance and Professionalism for Surveyors." **Professional and Technical Standards = Ch. 138, Subchapter E (§§138.81 et seq.).** Verified sections:
  - **§138.81** — General/applicability (professional surveying "shall meet or exceed" these standards; applies to any survey delineating/segregating/partitioning a real-property interest, except subdivision plats under §138.93).
  - **§138.83** — **Precision and Accuracy** (traverse/positional tolerances; closure).
  - **§138.87** — **Monumentation**.
  - **§138.91** — **Survey Drawing / Written Description / Report**.
  - **§138.93** — **Subdivision Plat**.
  - **§138.95** — **Descriptions Prepared for Political Subdivisions**.
  - ⚠️ Intermediate numbers exist (e.g., a definitions/survey-categories section ≈ 138.82); the exact section enumerating the survey categories was not independently confirmed — verify on the TBPELS rules PDF before citing.
- **"Category 1A" land-title / boundary survey:** the **highest-standard** boundary/land-title survey in Texas. The categories are defined by the **Texas Society of Professional Surveyors (TSPS) Manual of Practice (current 2024 ed.)**, which the TBPELS rules incorporate by reference: a Category 1A plat must bear a **certification of substantial compliance** with the current TSPS Manual requirements for a Category 1A Land Title Survey; a separate **closure report** is required.
- **Monument-setting (§138.87):** monuments set by an RPLS "shall be set at sufficient depth to retain a stable and distinctive location and be of sufficient size to withstand the deteriorating forces of nature"; the surveyor must "set, or leave as found, an adequate quantity of monuments of a stable and reasonably permanent nature to represent or reference the property or boundary corners," and set monuments "shall be marked in a way that is traceable to the responsible registrant or associated employer." Found vs. set monuments must be documented (§§138.87, 138.91(e)).
- **RPLS licensure:** a **Registered Professional Land Surveyor (RPLS)** is licensed by TBPELS (§1071.002; qualifications §1071.254). Only an RPLS (or LSLS) may prepare a metes-and-bounds description, delineate/monument boundaries, and seal/sign the survey.

**(b) Conventions.** Texas formally tiers surveys (Category 1A, 1B, etc.) via the TSPS Manual rather than relying solely on the national ALTA/NSPS standard (Texas surveyors often perform an ALTA/NSPS Land Title Survey *to also* satisfy Texas Category 1A). The **LSLS** is a Texas-specific credential for state-land work.

> **Texas conventions / vs. common law:** Post-2019, engineering and surveying boards merged into **TBPELS**, and the surveying standards moved from **22 TAC Ch. 663 → 22 TAC Ch. 138**. Citing "§663" in 2026 is outdated. Using a **state-society manual (TSPS)** incorporated by reference into the administrative code to define survey categories is a notable Texas regulatory mechanism.

## 7.6 Recording & plat requirements
**(a) Standards.**
- **Subdivision platting — Local Government Code Ch. 212 (§212.004, "Plat Required").** An owner who divides a tract within a municipality or its ETJ into two or more parts to lay out a subdivision, lots, streets, etc., **must have a plat prepared.** The statute expressly states a division "includes a division … made by using a **metes and bounds description** in a deed of conveyance or in a contract for deed … or by using any other method." The plat must be **filed and recorded with the county clerk** of the county where the tract lies, and **acknowledged** as for a deed. (Common exception: divisions into parts **greater than five acres** where each has access and no public improvement is dedicated.)
- **Recording — Property Code Ch. 12 (§12.002, "Subdivision Plat; Penalty").** A county clerk **may not record a plat/replat** unless approved as required by law by the appropriate authority and accompanied by the documents required by the Local Government Code — including an **original tax certificate** from each taxing unit showing no delinquent ad valorem taxes. General recording of instruments affecting real property is governed by Property Code Title 3, Ch. 12.

**(b) Conventions.** A standalone metes-and-bounds conveyance (e.g., a deed for a larger rural tract not creating a subdivision) is recorded under Property Code Ch. 12 without a plat; subdivisions trigger the Ch. 212 plat/approval/recording chain. Municipal and county subdivision rules (and ETJ reach) shape when a metes-and-bounds split is permissible without platting.

> **Texas conventions / vs. common law:** Texas allows conveyance by metes-and-bounds even within a regulated area, but **subjects that very method to the platting requirement** (§212.004) to prevent "metes-and-bounds" circumvention of subdivision control — an explicit statutory hook many states handle differently. Recording prerequisites (tax certificate, governmental approval) are Texas-specific gatekeeping at the county-clerk stage (§12.002).
