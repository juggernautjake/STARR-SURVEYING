# Core Rules

## Key Concepts

### Actions

Creatures can take up to three actions per turn. Some activities take up two or three actions and are considered to be two-action or three-action activities.

Many activities that fall under the category of spellcasting, including Divine Blessing, Redistribution, and more, provoke reactions from threatening enemies.

Action List
- InteractAs an action, a character may interact with an object. Some examples of this are listed below.Draw a weapon or other item from a pack.Open and step through an unlocked door.Pick up an item lying in the same hex as the creature.Activate a magic item.
- StepAs an action, a creature may move 5 feet. This does not provoke reactions from threatening creatures.See Movement for more information.
- StrikeAs an action, a creature may make an attack with a held weapon. See Attacks for more information.
- StrideAs an action, a creature may move up to their movement speed. Most medium creatures have a starting movement speed for 20 feet.See Movement for more information.

Holding Actions

A creature may hold an action by declaring that they will take an action when a trigger has occurred. A creature may only hold one activity at a time, or else they need to delay their entire turn. If the triggering event does not occur before the creature’s next turn, the action is lost.

Example:

An Arcanist declares that they are going to wait to launch their Elemental Blast until a creature enters or exits a chosen hex. Upon this trigger, the Arcanist uses their Elemental Blast attack as a two-action activity.

Reactions

Each creature gets one reaction per turn. Reactions cannot be used unless provoked; most abilities that use a reaction specify how the reaction is provoked.

Actions that provoke reactions from threatening opponents
- Striding through a threatened square.
- Ranged attacks.
- Spellcasting, including Redistribution, Divine Blessing, and similar abilities.
- Using an untrained combat skill.Only the creature who was targeted with the combat skill is provoked.

When a reaction is provoked, a creature may take either of the actions listed below:
- Step
- Strike This strike may be an attack with a weapon or an attack with any trained combat skill.

Skill Checks

Skills require at least one action to perform, though many require multiple actions. Consult the ability used for each skill to learn more about what actions are required to make a skill check.

Support Action

Once per turn, a creature may attempt to aid their ally in an activity by rolling the same check before the ally makes their attempt. This is normally limited to skill checks. A character may only use the Support action if they are trained in the skill used.

The Support action does not convey advantage.
- Combat SkillsAs an action, a character may attempt to assist their ally on an upcoming combat skill check against a target they both threaten. The ally may use the die roll from this assist check in place of their own die roll on the check if they choose to.
- General SkillsAs an action, a character may attempt to assist their ally on an upcoming general skill check. The ally may use the die roll from this assist check in place of their own die roll on the check if they choose to.

### Attacks

An attack roll is opposed by the reflex save of the target. Use D20’s for both of these checks.

Attacks are calculated using the creature’s dexterity modifier, plus any additional modifiers as necessary.

If a character is proficient with the weapon used, they add a bonus equal to their level on the attack roll.

Some weapons require a certain amount of strength to wield.

If you use multiple actions to attack in one round, each following attack takes a -2 penalty. This is called the Multiple Strike Penalty (MSP).

Flanking

If you can draw a line between the center of your square and the center of an Ally's square and there is a creature between the two of you whom you both threaten, you are flanking that creature.

Flanking grants a +2 bonus on attack rolls for all flanking creatures.

### Advantage
- Advantage is a term used to describe when a character is being credited with a bonus on a certain check. It is the opposite of disadvantage.
- When a creature has advantage, they roll the d20 two times and use the better result.
- Disadvantage works in the opposite manner. The d20 is rolled twice, and the less favorable result is used.
- Multiple sources of advantage
- If a creature has multiple sources of advantage on the same check, they do not roll the dice more than two times. Instead, their result is automatically treated as one step higher on the degrees of success chart.
- Disadvantage functions the same way as advantage for this, except that the check is automatically treated as one step lower on the degrees of success chart.
- Additional sources of advantage and disadvantage move the result of the check up and down on the degrees of success chart as would be appropriate.
- Eg: Player A has advantage on an attack roll because they are using the Offensive Stance. They also have advantage from using the Focused Strike feat. Regardless of the result of their roll as compared to their opponent’s reflex save, their success is treated as one degree higher when attacking NPC B.
- Conflicting advantage
- If two creatures have conflicting bonuses on the degrees of success chart after using advantage, the conflicting bonuses and penalties cancel each other out.
- Eg: Player A has the same benefits to his attack as described above. NPC B is using the defensive stance and has a unique power granting them advantage on reflex saves against people using the offensive stance. Both players roll with advantage, and then the impacts to the degrees of success chart cancel each other out. If NPC B did not have the unique power, then both players would roll with advantage and Player A would move one step higher on the chart as normal.

### Criticals

A critical can be achieved if one roll total is 20 higher than the opposing roll total. It can otherwise be achieved when a success is rolled and the character who succeeded had multiple sources of advantage.

A critical failure is the opposite consequence of a critical success.

### Healing

Heal checks are the basic way to care for someone's wounds

When not engaged in rigorous activity, characters heal at a rate of 2 HP + their Constitution modifier per hour from nonlethal damage.

Characters heal from lethal damage at a rate of 2 HP + their Constitution modifier per day.

Characters can also be healed using some abilities and with the Heal skill.

## Gameplay

### Cover
- Cover is determined based on line of effect based on a straight line drawn from the hex of origin to any hex that the creature is in.
- Cover is stackable. A creature may have full coverage and visibility coverage from remaining behind a wall even if the attacker knows the creature is there.
- Cover of any kind negates Sneak Attack damage.
- Coverage Types
- Impassable Coverage
- A creature with impassable coverage automatically critically succeeds all Reflex Saves. This means that the barrier must be removed before an attack will affect them.
- Common Sources
- Being inside a building with no viable paths for an attack from outside of the building to affect you.
- Full Coverage
- A creature with full coverage gains double advantage on reflex saves and stealth checks.
- Common Sources
- Remaining behind a barrier that ends at or above their height.
- Partial Coverage
- A creature with partial coverage gains a +2 bonus on reflex saves and stealth checks. This bonus can stack with a shield bonus or multiple sources of partial coverage.
- Common Sources
- Remaining behind a barrier that ends below their height.
- Sharing a square with another creature of the same size.
- Sharing a square without the Welcoming Stance requires a DC 15 Acrobatics check from both creatures. Failure results in both creatures falling prone.
- When a barrier intersects with one corner of a hex that is part of the line of effect.
- Visibility Coverage
- A creature with visibility coverage gains advantage on reflex saves and stealth checks. Visibility coverage does not apply when the target creature is flat-footed.
- Common Sources
- Invisibility.
- Darkness.

**Damage types**

Physical

All physical damage is subject to DR.

Physical damage does not affect incorporeal creatures.

There are three types of physical damage, listed below.

Piercing

Bludgeoning

Slashing

Bleed

Bleed damage is not subject to DR.

Bleed damage does not affect incorporeal creatures.

Bleed damage is recurring. It can only be stopped using the Heal skill, or it stops automatically if the creature regains hit points from magic or an item.

Elemental

All elemental damage is subject to DR.

Elemental damage does affect incorporeal creatures.

There are five types of elemental damage, listed below.

Acid

Corrodes materials

Any armor that prevents acid damage can take a maximum amount of damage equal to its DR squared.

Armor granting a +4 bonus can withstand 16 points of acid damage before taking the broken condition.

Fire

Fire effects leave metal hot for 1d6 rounds after the damage is done. Any creature touching something made of metal takes 1d6 points of damage on any turn that the metal has been directly exposed to fire damage.

If a creature is wearing metal armor or clothing and is hit by a fire effect, they take 1d6 points of damage. The DR provided by the armor does not apply to this damage.

Fire damage ignites any easily flammable sources, such as straw or oil. Any creature in or adjacent to a square that is on fire takes 1d6 points of damage every round.

Cold

Cold damage is particularly difficult to recover from. If a creature takes cold damage, they are at disadvantage on their fortitude save from damage that rouind.

Electricity

Electricity ignores metal armor's DR and travels through water, dealing damage to all creatures within 5 squares of the lightning effect if they are touching the water.

If a creature in the water makes an attack using electricity, they automatically take full damage from that attack.

Sonic

Sonic effects require their victim to make a fortitude save against the attack roll. If the save fails, they lose their hearing for 1 round.

This is separate from the Fortitude Save required for taking damage.

Force

Fall damage is a type of force damage.

Not subject to DR.

Affects incorporeal creatures.

Plasma

Rare in most settings.

Does not affect incorporeal creatures.

Plasma damages everything that it touches. Every time that an object is subjected to plasma damage, it loses 1 HP.

Nonlethal

Not subject to DR.

Affects incorporeal creatures.

If a creature is at less than 0 hit points, nonlethal damage does not affect them. If they are still able to take actions because of an ability and they take nonlethal damage, they immediately fall unconscious.

Nonlethal damage can only be healed through resting for an hour or more. Even spirit based healing does not heal nonlethal damage unless it explicitly says otherwise.

### Movement

**Character Movement**

Walk

A walk represents standard non-tactical movement (3 miles per hour for an unencumbered medium-sized character).

Creatures cannot heal from nonlethal damage while walking.

Hustle

A hustle is a jog (about 6 miles per hour for an unencumbered medium-sized character).

This is a rigorous activity that causes the creature to take 1 point of nonlethal damage per 10 minutes.

Tactical Movement

Tactical movement is used for combat. This is a highly rigorous activity that causes the creature to take 1 point of nonlethal damage per 1 minute.

Stride

Standard tactical movement using a single action is called a Stride, and most people move 20 feet with a single Stride action.

Run

If a character spends all three actions on their turn making Strides and goes roughly in a straight line, they move an additional 20 feet this turn.

**Mounted Movement**

When on a mount, a creature may direct their mount using an action. The mount then takes two actions to move or attack as directed, and the character may take the rest of their turn as normal.

If using a mount on a longer trek, the mount may move at the speeds listed below:

Walk

The creature covers a distance equal to half their move speed every round.

Trot

The creature covers a distance equal to their move speed every round.

Canter

The creature covers a distance equal to double their move speed every round.

Gallop

The creature covers a distance equal to triple their move speed every round.

## Miscellaenous

### Aging

Child

If your age is 14 or younger, your ability scores vary wildly. It is recommended not to play a character this young, but children’s scores should be calculated uniquely and should never include physical stats as high as an adult.

Young Adult

If your age is 34 or younger, but not a child, your ability scores do not change because of your age.

Mature Adult

If your age is (34 years + 1d6 years) you take a penalty to one physical ability score and increase one mental ability score.

Middle Aged

If your age is (44 years + 1d6 years) you take a penalty to one physical ability score, increase one mental ability score, and account for earlier aging.

Old

If your age is (54 years + 1d6 years) roll 1d4 and assign that many penalties to your choice of ability scores. Increase one mental ability score and account for earlier aging.

Venerable

Every 10 years after “Old”, roll 1d6 to determine how many ability scores take a penalty. Assign as necessary and account for earlier aging.

### Encumbrance

A character can become encumbered due to the heavy weight that they are carrying. There is a chart below that determines encumbrance based on your strength score. These values are tripled for quadrupeds.

Comfortable Held Weight refers to the maximum weight that a creature can carry in their arms while taking stride actions. If this weight is exceeded, the creature can no longer take the stride action and must use the step action to move.

Comfortable Carry Weight refers to the maximum weight that a creature can carry on their body while taking stride actions. If this weight is exceeded, the creature can no longer take the stride action and must use the step action to move.

Maximum Held Weight refers to the maximum weight that a creature can carry in their arms while taking step actions. If this weight is exceeded, the creature cannot move with this weight in their arms and drops it.

Maximum Carry Weight refers to the maximum weight that a creature can carry on their body while taking step actions. If this weight is exceeded, the creature cannot move with this weight and falls prone.

Maximum Drag Weight refers to the maximum weight that a creature can push or pull. When doing this, the creature must use the Drag combat skill or make a Strength check. If the maximum drag weight is not exceeded, then the creature divides the results of their check by 5 and can move that distance with the dragged item.
